Measuring Effort

How do you measure your workout? Dp you record calories burned or distance covered? Maybe you record the weights lifted in sets or simply time spent in the gym? That works for some, but not if you're a CrossFitter, doing P90x or working through a strength and conditioning program.

How It Works

It's based on sound scientific principals not some guru fluff (warning science ahead).

The Power of Joules

The joule, a derived unit of energy or work, is equal to the energy expended in applying a force of one newton through a distance of one meter. In other words, joules is a measure of power output. If you generate enough power to produce one joule per second you generate one watt. So joules over time is described as watts and that's what were measuring for you.

To calculate joules the formula is simple enough — joules = (mass x acceleration) x distance. Distance, measured in meteres, represents how far something moved. Mass is the weight of the object in kilograms. We're representing Acceleration as the constant value of 9.8. The 9.8 constant represents the fight against gravity or 9.8 meters/second/second.

To derives watts from joules you simply divide the joules by the number of seconds the activity took. That’s a very brief overview but covers the bases. If you’re interested in learning the details in this formula you can read more about the particulars in the Work section of this article.

How About An Example

For an example of how we calculate joules and watts we'll need to start with some basic stats. Let's assume the subject of our calculation weighs and even 70kg, and performs a simple jump of 1/2 meters straight up. The subject performs the jump conveniently in exactly one second.

Based on our formula watts = ((mass x acceleration) x distance) / time we know the subject generated 343w for that one second. Here is the breakdown of the above calculation (Remember joules = (mass x acceleration) x distance:

  • 343 joules = (70kg * 9.8) * .5m
  • 343 watts = 343 joules/1 second

    • If you could capture that energy in a battery with 100% efficiency you would have enough to power a 100w light bulb for 3 seconds. I'm sure you're thinking that's a pretty simplistic example, and it is, but using that same methodology we can also calculate the watts in a deadlift, burpee, bench press -- anything really, the difficulty is gathering the information for the variable in the equation.

    Gathering the Variables

    Before we can apply the calculation we need to get some stats together. Distance traveled may seems simple at first blush but it can get complicated. For example how far does the weight travel in a deadlift? Of course that is different for everyone but fortunately we're not as unique as we like to think. There are standards in our human physique we can apply. For example, did you know the distance from the ground to your hip is ~53% of your height?

    Using these standards (and knowing your height) we are able to compute distance for most exercises. Let’s look at the pullup as an example. A 1.8m tall individual roughly has a distance from his forearm to his shoulder of 33.2% of his height or .60m and that is the distance he would travel in a pullup.

    Mass follows the same pricipals as distance. Certain standards apply to the weight of different parts of our physique just like it does for height. In the case of a pullup it is 100% of your weight because you are essentially lifting your entire body but that isn’t true for all exercises. What about a situp? All we need is calculate the added weight of our head, torso and arms (53% of your body weight).

    Once you have the method for getting distance and mass for each exercise for any individual, and we do, you just plug in the numbers to the formula.
